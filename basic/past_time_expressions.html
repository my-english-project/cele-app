01

Time Adverb / Noun

Yesterday

The day immediately before today — always used with the Simple Past.

"Yesterday" refers to the day immediately before today. It is a relative term — if today is Wednesday, yesterday is Tuesday. Because it refers to a completed period, it is used exclusively with the Simple Past.
Combination Meaning
yesterday morning Sunrise to noon on the previous day
yesterday afternoon Noon to ~5:00 PM on the previous day
yesterday evening 5:00 PM until bedtime on the previous day
the day before yesterday Two days ago
"Yesterday" usually appears at the very beginning or very end of a sentence.

[Subject] + [Past Verb] + [Object] + yesterday
I bought a new car yesterday. Yesterday, + [Subject] + [Past Verb] + [Object]
Yesterday, the weather was beautiful.
Rule 1 — The "Night" exception. We do not say "yesterday night."
I watched a movie yesterday night.
I watched a movie last night.
Rule 2 — No prepositions. Unlike months or dates, "yesterday" needs no preposition.
On yesterday, I went to the park.
Yesterday, I went to the park.
Rule 3 — Reported speech. In reported speech, "yesterday" becomes "the previous day."
Direct: "I arrived yesterday."
Reported: "He said he had arrived the previous day."
  • 01"I finished the entire project yesterday afternoon."
  • 02"Yesterday was a very long and stressful day at the office."
  • 03"We met the day before yesterday at the conference."
  • 04"I didn't see you at the gym yesterday morning; were you late?"
  • 05"What did you eat for dinner yesterday?"
02

Determiner

Last

The most recently completed version of a time period — Simple Past.

"Last" points to the previous unit of time relative to today. Because that period is finished, it is almost always used with the Simple Past. It is extremely versatile — it pairs with virtually any calendar unit or day.
Interval Common Phrases
Days last Monday, last Tuesday, last weekend
Months last January, last month, last June
Seasons last summer, last winter, last spring
Special last night, last year, last century
"Last" always comes before the time noun. Never use an article ("the") before "last" when it is a time marker.

Last + [Time Noun]
I saw a movie last night. / We traveled to Japan last year. I went to Paris the last week.
I went to Paris last week.
"Last Night" vs. "Yesterday Night" — "Last night" is always standard.
I slept poorly last night.
"Last week" vs. "In the last week"
I was sick last week. → The previous calendar week (Simple Past).
I have been sick in the last week. → 7 days up to right now (Present Perfect).
Last vs. Next — Opposite directions on the timeline.
Last Monday — the Monday that just passed.
Next Monday — the Monday that is coming soon.
  • 01"I started my new job last Monday, and I'm really enjoying it."
  • 02"Did you hear that thunderstorm last night? It was very loud."
  • 03"We went skiing last winter, but this year we are staying home."
  • 04"I haven't seen her since last month's meeting."
  • 05"Revenue increased significantly last year."
03

Adverb — position: AFTER the time period

Ago

Counts backward from the present moment. Exclusively Simple Past.

"Ago" transforms a period of time into a specific point in history, always counting back from now. Because it refers to a finished action at a specific past time, it is used almost exclusively with the Simple Past.
Range Examples
Short a moment ago, a few minutes ago, an hour ago
Medium four days ago, two weeks ago, a month ago
Long decades ago, centuries ago, a long time ago
Unlike most time markers, "ago" always comes after the time measurement.

[Time Period] + ago
Two weeks ago. / Ten minutes ago. [Subject] + [Past Verb] + [Time Period] + ago
I graduated three years ago.
  • 01"You just missed him; he left the office five minutes ago."
  • 02"Dinosaurs became extinct millions of years ago."
  • 03"We started this project a few months ago, and it's nearly finished."
  • 04"Long ago, in a land far away, there lived a king."
04

Demonstrative Determiners

This / That

Psychological and temporal distance from the present moment.

"This" (Immediate/Recent Past): Refers to a period still belonging to the current cycle — today, this week, this month. It feels "close" to the present.
"That" (Remote/Specific Past): Refers to a specific point in the more distant past, or a time already introduced in a narrative. It creates "distance".
Marker Phrases Context
this this morning, this afternoon, this Monday Within the same day or week
that that day, that year, that summer Storytelling or historical reference
Feature This That
Distance Near / Current Far / Finished
Atmosphere Immediate / Personal Narrative / Historical
Common Tense Sim. Past / Pres. Perfect Sim. Past / Past Perfect
Example I saw him this afternoon. I saw him that afternoon in 1999.
The Storytelling "That" — When narrating the past, use "that" to signal distance from the present.
"I moved to London in 2015. That year was the most exciting of my life."
"This" for the Very Recent Past — Use "this" if the larger time unit is still ongoing.
"I've been very busy this week." (The week is still happening.)
  • 01"I have already finished two reports this morning." (It is currently 11:00 AM).
  • 02"My grandparents met in 1950. That summer was unusually hot."
  • 03"Did you hear the loud noise this morning?"
  • 04"I lost my keys on Tuesday. That day was a complete nightmare."
05

Indefinite Time Marker — Spoken English

The Other

Vague, conversational reference to a recent but unspecified past day.

"The other + [time noun]" signals a time close to the present where the exact date either isn't remembered or doesn't matter. Very common in spoken, conversational English. Used with the Simple Past.
Phrase Approximate Meaning
the other day 2 to 5 days ago
the other week 1 to 3 weeks ago
the other night A recent evening (often storytelling)
the other month 1 to 2 months ago (less common)
Vagueness is the point. Don't use "the other" if you want to be precise.
Precise: "I saw him on Tuesday." / Vague: "I saw him the other day."
Not for the distant past.
I graduated university the other year (in 2010).
I graduated university a few years ago.
"The other day" (past) vs. "Another day" (future/different).
"I saw her the other day." → past event.
"Let's meet another day." → future or a different day.
  • 01"I was walking downtown the other day when I saw that new cafe."
  • 02"We had a really strange dream the other night."
  • 03"I think I left my umbrella at your house the other week."
  • 04"I saw a beautiful sunrise the other morning while jogging."
  • 05"Someone called you the other day, but didn't leave a message."
06

Preposition / Conjunction vs. Adverb

Before / Earlier

One needs a reference anchor; the other stands alone.

"Before" is a preposition or conjunction. It requires a reference point — an event, time, or action — to follow it. It answers: Before what?
"Earlier" is the comparative adverb of "early." It means "at a time before now" or "before the time previously mentioned." It can stand alone (typically meaning earlier today).
Phrase Usage
before then Before a specific past moment
earlier today Some time ago within the current day
years before Long prior to a specific event
much earlier Significantly before expected time
Before + [Noun / -ing / Clause]
I arrived before the meeting. / Wash your hands before eating. / I left before it started to rain.
Earlier — standalone or with This/That
I saw him earlier. (= earlier today)
I finished the task earlier this morning.
Feature Before Earlier
Grammar Preposition / Conjunction Adverb
Reference Needs a specific anchor point Refers to "now" or "then"
Standalone? Only to mean "ever in my life" Yes — usually "earlier today"
vs. Ago Counts from another past event —
Example Call me before you leave. I called you earlier.
  • 01"Please finish the report before Friday."
  • 02"I went to the bank earlier, but it was closed."
  • 03"Have we met before? Your face looks familiar."
  • 04"The meeting was at 3:00, but we started earlier."
  • 05"Check the tires before you start the long drive."
07

Adverbs of Completed States

Once / At One Time / Formerly

States from the past now completely over — varying in formality.

"Once" — Something was true at an unspecified past time; a single state or long period that is now completely over.
"At one time" — Very similar to "once," but emphasizes a specific (though unnamed) period or stage in someone's life.
"Formerly" — The most formal option. Describes a previous status, title, name, or function. Common in professional or historical contexts.
Phrase Combination Context
once upon a time Start of stories Narrative / Fiction
formerly known as Name changes Professional / Official
at one time or another Universal experiences Idiomatic
Feature Once Formerly
Formality Low / Medium (conversational) High (professional)
Focus General memory or state Previous title or function
Placement Before verb / sentence start Before noun / adjective
Example I once had a cat. He is a formerly famous actor.
"Once" vs. "One time"
Once → continuous state: "I once had long hair."
One time → single specific event: "One time, I fell off my bike."
Formal "Formerly" — Avoid in casual speech; use "used to" or "once" instead.
Casual: "I used to work at the mall."
Formal: "I was formerly employed as a retail consultant."
The "Used to" Connection — All three often pair with used to + Verb to emphasize the habit has stopped.
"At one time, I used to smoke, but I quit years ago."
  • 01"This neighborhood was once very quiet, but now it is full of traffic."
  • 02"At one time, scientists believed the earth was flat."
  • 03"Istanbul was formerly known as Constantinople."
  • 04"I once met the president at a charity event."
  • 05"The formerly independent company was acquired by a large corporation."
08

Prepositions of Time

On / In

Anchors for specific time periods — scale determines the choice.

"In" — Used for larger, enclosed, or non-specific periods: months, years, centuries, and seasons.
"On" — Used for specific, smaller units of time: days of the week and exact dates.
Preposition Category Examples
in Months in January, in October
in Years in 1988, in the 90s
in Seasons in the summer, in winter
on Days on Monday, on my birthday
on Dates on May 5th, on the 1st of July
Feature In On
Time Scale Long / General Short / Specific
Calendar View The whole month / year A single day / date
Typical Nouns 2024, Summer, April Tuesday, Anniversary, 12th
Example We met in July. We met on July 4th.
Parts of the Day — "In" for general periods; "On" when you specify which day.
I usually study in the morning. / I had a meeting on Monday morning.
The "At" Exception — Use "in" for morning/afternoon/evening, but "at" for night.
It was cold at night.
Holidays — "At" for the whole period; "On" for the specific day.
I saw them at Christmas. / I saw them on Christmas Day.
  • 01"The Berlin Wall fell in 1989."
  • 02"I started my current job on January 15th."
  • 03"We moved to this city in the spring of last year."
  • 04"Were you at the office on Friday?"
  • 05"I was born in June, specifically on a Sunday."
09

Adverbs of Recency

Previously / Recently / Lately

Three degrees of "not long ago" — formality, tense, and repetition set them apart.

Previously
Refers to a time before a specific point. Formal writing. Describes a former state or sequence of events.
Months or years ago.
Recently
The near past — a short time ago. Works for a single finished action or a state that began not long ago.
Days or weeks ago.
Lately
A repeated action or continuing state that started in the near past and is still relevant now.
A few weeks ago until today.
Previously — contrasts a past state with the present.
He works in sales now, but previously he was a teacher.
Recently — pairs with Simple Past (one-time) or Present Perfect.
I bought a new car recently. (Simple Past)
I have visited London recently. (Present Perfect)
Lately — almost exclusively Present Perfect or Pres. Perfect Continuous. Common in questions and negatives.
I have been feeling tired lately. / Have you seen any movies lately?
Position of "Lately" — Usually at the end of the sentence.
I haven't talked to her lately.
"Previously" vs. "Before" — Previously is more formal.
Informal: "I've seen this before." / Formal: "As previously stated…"
One-time Events — Cannot use "lately" for a single completed action.
I went to the dentist lately.
I went to the dentist recently.
  • 01"He works in sales now, but previously he was a teacher."
  • 02"The company recently announced a change in its policy."
  • 03"Have you noticed any changes in the weather lately?"
  • 04"We went to that new Italian restaurant recently; it was great."
  • 05"I haven't been sleeping very well lately due to the stress."
