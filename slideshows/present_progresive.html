01 · Definition & Use

What is the Present Progressive?

Also called Present Continuous — describes actions in progress at the moment of speaking, temporary situations, and future arrangements.

The Present Progressive (also called Present Continuous) is formed with the verb To Be in the present + the -ing form of the main verb. It expresses actions that are happening at the time of speaking, temporary situations, and planned future events.
Key Idea: The action is in progress right now — it started before this moment and has not finished yet. This is different from the Simple Present, which describes habits or permanent truths.
Formula: Subject + am / is / are + verb-ing
"She is reading a book right now." · "They are dancing in the street."
Use 1 — Action happening right now:
"Shh! The baby is sleeping." · "I am writing an email."
Use 2 — Temporary situation (not permanent):
"She is living with her parents this month." (not forever)
Use 3 — Planned future arrangement:
"We are meeting the director tomorrow at 10."
Use 4 — Changing or developing situation:
"Prices are rising every month." · "Her English is improving a lot."
Signal Examples
Right now right now, at this moment, at the moment
Currently currently, nowadays, these days
Today today, this morning, this week
Look / Listen Look! · Listen! · Watch out!
Still still (ongoing action)
Future use tomorrow, next week, on Saturday (with plans)
  • 01"I am playing soccer right now."
  • 02"The bird is singing in the garden."
  • 03"We are dancing at the party."
  • 04"Look! It is raining!"
  • 05"She is studying for the exam this week."
  • 06"They are travelling to Mexico next Friday."

02 · Sentence Structure

Affirmative Form

Subject + am / is / are + verb-ing — same rule for all persons.

Affirmative Formula
Subject + am / is / are + verb-ing
Contractions are very common in spoken English:
I am → I'm  ·  She is → She's  ·  They are → They're
Subject To Be Example
I am I am studying English.
He / She / It is She is cooking dinner.
You are You are doing great.
We are We are working now.
They are They are playing outside.
  • 01"I am having breakfast right now."
  • 02"She is reading a novel this week."
  • 03"The butterflies are flying high."
  • 04"We are looking for a shirt."
  • 05"It is raining in London."
  • 06"This man is drinking tea."

03 · Sentence Structure

Negative Form

Add not after am / is / are — contractions are common in everyday speech.

Negative Formula
Subject + am/is/are + not + verb-ing
Two contractions are possible — both are correct:
He isn't reading.  =  He's not reading.
They aren't dancing.  =  They're not dancing.
Subject Full form Contraction
I am not reading 'm not reading
He / She / It is not reading isn't / 's not reading
You / We / They are not reading aren't / 're not reading
Mistake 1 — "not" in the wrong position:
She is reading not the book.
She is not reading the book.
Mistake 2 — Using "don't / doesn't" instead of "isn't / aren't":
He doesn't eating now.
He isn't eating now.
Mistake 3 — Forgetting -ing in negatives:
They aren't dance right now.
They aren't dancing right now.
  • 01"I am not reading now." / "I'm not reading now."
  • 02"He is not reading. / He's not reading."
  • 03"She isn't watching TV — she's studying."
  • 04"You are not running fast enough."
  • 05"We aren't looking for a hat."
  • 06"This man isn't drinking water."

04 · Interrogative Form

Question Form

Invert am / is / are and the subject — no auxiliary "do/does" needed.

Yes/No Formula
Am / Is / Are + Subject + verb-ing + ?
Question Short Answer ✅ Short Answer ❌
Am I doing this right? Yes, you are. No, you aren't.
Is she reading now? Yes, she is. No, she isn't.
Are they running? Yes, they are. No, they aren't.
Wh- Formula
Wh-word + am/is/are + Subject + verb-ing + ?
Question Example Answer
What are you doing? I'm writing a report.
Where are they going? They're going to the cinema.
Why is she crying? Because she's watching a sad film.
Who is he talking to? He's talking to the manager.
Rule: In short answers, repeat the auxiliary (am / is / are). Never use a contraction in a positive short answer.

Is she working? — Yes, she's.
Is she working? — Yes, she is.

Is she working? — No, she isn't. ✓
Mistake 1 — Using "do/does" to form questions:
Does she is reading?
Is she reading?
Mistake 2 — Wrong word order in Wh- questions:
What you are doing?
What are you doing?
Mistake 3 — Affirmative short answer with contraction:
Are they coming? — Yes, they're.
Are they coming? — Yes, they are.

05 · Orthography

Spelling Rules for -ing

It is not always just adding "-ing" — six rules govern how the continuous form is written.

1

Standard → just add -ing

For most verbs ending in a consonant, simply add -ing to the base form.

do → doing  ·  ask → asking  ·  play → playing  ·  read → reading

2

Silent -e → drop the -e, then add -ing

When the verb ends in a silent -e, remove it before adding -ing.

make → making  ·  take → taking  ·  write → writing  ·  dance → dancing

3

Short CVC verbs → double the consonant + -ing

For one-syllable verbs ending in Consonant-Vowel-Consonant, double the final consonant before adding -ing.

swim → swimming  ·  run → running  ·  sit → sitting  ·  hit → hitting

4

Ends in -w, -x or -y → just add -ing

Verbs ending in -w, -x, or -y never double the final letter.

enjoy → enjoying  ·  study → studying  ·  fix → fixing  ·  show → showing

5

Two-syllable CVC (stressed last syllable) → double + -ing

When a two-syllable verb ends in CVC and the last syllable is stressed, double the final consonant.

commit → committing  ·  begin → beginning  ·  forget → forgetting

6

Two-syllable CVC (stress on 1st syllable) → just add -ing

When the first syllable is stressed, do NOT double the final consonant.

whis·per → whispering  ·  lis·ten → listening  ·  hap·pen → happening

Base Verb Rule -ing Form Reason
ask Rule 1 asking standard → +ing
make Rule 2 making silent -e → drop e
run Rule 3 running CVC, 1 syllable → double n
study Rule 4 studying ends in -y → +ing
begin Rule 5 beginning CVC, stress on 2nd → double n
whisper Rule 6 whispering CVC, stress on 1st → +ing
die Special dying -ie → change to y

06 · Advanced Note

Stative Verbs — Do Not Use -ing

Some verbs describe states, not actions — they are rarely used in the progressive form.

Stative verbs describe permanent states: feelings, thoughts, possession, senses. They do not describe an action happening right now, so they are not normally used in progressive tenses.
I am knowing the answer right now.
I know the answer.
She is loving chocolate.
She loves chocolate.
Feelings / Emotions
love · hate · like · prefer · want · need · wish
Thoughts / Mental states
know · believe · think · understand · remember · forget
Senses & Possession
see · hear · smell · taste · have · own · belong
Some verbs can be both stative AND dynamic — the meaning changes:

think: I think you're right. (opinion = stative)
I am thinking about the problem. (mental process = dynamic)

have: She has a car. (possession = stative)
She is having lunch. (activity = dynamic)

see: I see the problem. (understand = stative)
I am seeing the doctor today. (appointment = dynamic)
